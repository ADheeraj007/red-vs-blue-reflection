Overview of the Lab Environment

The capstone project was designed to simulate a small enterprise network using virtual infrastructure. Our team deployed Proxmox as the hypervisor, a VyOS router to manage WAN/LAN routing, pfSense as the perimeter firewall, an Ubuntu server hosting the Wazuh Manager and indexer, and a Windows 10 workstation as the primary monitored endpoint. The network was intentionally segmented and isolated to reduce risk while still allowing realistic routing, firewall, and logging scenarios.

Within this structure, every team acted as both attacker and defender. As the blue side, we were responsible for securing and monitoring our own Proxmox-based environment. As part of the course red team, we participated in controlled exercises that targeted other teams’ environments - not our own - with the goal of simulating realistic adversary behavior under faculty supervision.

After observing the results of these attacks, we treated them as case studies and asked: “If the same techniques were used against our Windows 10 host, what would Sysmon record, and which Wazuh rules should alert?” This closed the loop between offensive activity and defensive visibility.

My Personal Focus as a Purple Teamer

My role in the capstone was intentionally “purple”: I contributed to selected red-team tasks while also helping interpret and strengthen blue-team defenses for our own lab. I was not the primary operator for every exploit or configuration, but I concentrated on several key areas:

  • Supporting SMB brute-force testing and login failure analysis during attacks on other teams’ environments.
  • Reviewing FTP access to a challenge server and examining files delivered to the attacker.
  • Helping tune and interpret Wazuh alerts tied to Sysmon Event IDs 1, 3, 11, and 13.
  • Contributing to hardening work on the Windows endpoint and validating that changes produced the expected logging patterns in our own environment.

This blend of responsibilities gave me a practical purple-team experience: I was able to understand the attacker’s workflow against peer teams while simultaneously considering exactly what defenders must be able to see in dashboards and logs to protect our own environment.

Key Techniques

Attack and Defense Techniques We Implemented

Selected examples from the broader workflow

Offensive Techniques We Practiced Against Other Teams

In the red-team portion of the course, we operated against other student teams’ lab environments rather than our own. This structure allowed everyone to experience being attacked and defended without self-targeting. We used that space to practice attack chains that map to common adversary behaviors. While I did not execute every individual step, we collectively worked through techniques such as:

  • SMB brute-force attempts: We used tools such as Metasploit and CrackMapExec to send repeated authentication attempts against SMB services on peer environments, observing STATUS_LOGON_FAILURE responses and tracking how quickly account lockout thresholds might be reached in a real environment.
  • FTP enumeration and retrieval: We authenticated to a FileZilla FTP server hosted by another team, identified accessible directories, and downloaded a challenge archive and the john.txt wordlist to support subsequent password-cracking exercises.
  • RPC and SMB enumeration: Using tools like rpcclient, smbmap, and Impacket’s smbclient.py, we enumerated users, shares, and permissions in target environments, highlighting how misconfigurations can inadvertently expose sensitive information.
  • Web service verification: We confirmed that target systems were hosting IIS web servers on HTTP and compared that exposure with filtered or blocked ports such as 22 and 443, reinforcing the importance of deliberate network service design.

Because these attacks were directed at other teams, they created a realistic adversarial pressure without jeopardizing our own lab. At the same time, they provided rich examples we later mapped back to our Wazuh and Sysmon telemetry: if this happened to us, which events and rules would we expect to see?

Defensive Techniques We Applied in Our Environment

The defensive side of the project focused on our responsibility as the blue team for our own Proxmox environment. We concentrated on building visibility, validating telemetry, and connecting raw events to higher-level security narratives. Key techniques included:

  • Endpoint onboarding and grouping: We deployed the Wazuh agent on our Windows 10 system and confirmed that the host appeared in the Endpoints view under the windows-sysmon group, providing confidence that telemetry was flowing.
  • Sysmon with a curated configuration: We installed Sysmon using the SwiftOnSecurity configuration so that process creation, file writes, registry edits, and network connections were captured with useful detail rather than noise.
  • Rule-based alerting in Wazuh: We examined how Sysmon events were translated into Wazuh rules, including examples such as:
    • Rule 92213: Executable files dropped in directories commonly abused by malware.
    • Rule 92307: Registry evidence of new service creation.
    • Rule 61605: Sysmon Event ID 3, signaling outbound network connections.
  • Mapping to MITRE ATT&CK: We used the Wazuh MITRE view to align alerts with tactics such as Command and Control, Privilege Escalation, and Defense Evasion, which helped us reason about attacker objectives rather than isolated events.

This defensive work demonstrated that a properly configured endpoint and SIEM can convert low-level system activity into actionable detections that security analysts can investigate under time pressure - especially when informed by firsthand experience launching attacks against other teams.

Lessons, Challenges, and Career Impact

Reflections on Learning and Teamwork

Technical growth and professional development

Lessons Learned and Challenges Faced

One of the most significant lessons was how sensitive an integrated lab is to configuration errors. A single incorrect gateway, a missing NAT rule, or an overly restrictive firewall policy in pfSense could completely disrupt communication between Proxmox, VyOS, Ubuntu, and Windows. Rather than treating these as minor inconveniences, we treated them as realistic troubleshooting scenarios that mirrored production network engineering work.

Another key realization was that visibility is not automatic. Installing Sysmon or a Wazuh agent does not guarantee effective detection. We had to verify that events were actually collected, that rules were firing as expected, and that alerts mapped clearly to attacker behaviors. This reinforced the importance of end-to-end validation rather than assuming that a nominally “deployed” tool is providing adequate coverage.

Finally, working across both offensive examples against other teams and defensive analysis in our own environment highlighted the value of thinking holistically. Understanding how an attacker chains reconnaissance, exploitation, and persistence steps together made it much easier to interpret alerts in context and to distinguish meaningful signals from background noise.

Teamwork, Decision-Making, and Career Development

Our team relied heavily on collaborative decision-making. One member focused on VyOS routing, another on pfSense firewall tuning, others on Wazuh configuration and documentation. We had to decide where to allocate time - whether to invest effort in troubleshooting connectivity, refining logging, or exploring additional attack paths against peer environments. This reflected the type of prioritization that security operations centers perform when not every task can be completed at once.

As a purple team participant, I gained experience translating offensive techniques into concrete defensive questions: if a similar brute-force or malware drop occurred on our Windows host, which Sysmon Event IDs would be triggered, and which Wazuh rules should highlight that behavior? Developing this habit of “thinking like an attacker, but acting like a defender” is directly relevant to roles in security operations, detection engineering, and incident response.

In terms of career development, this project gives me more than a list of tools on a résumé. It provides a cohesive story: I helped design and operate an isolated Proxmox environment, participated in offensive exercises against other teams’ environments, and used Wazuh and Sysmon data to reason about detections and hardening in our own lab. This experience strengthens my trajectory toward a purple-team or blue-team position where I can investigate alerts, tune rules, and communicate findings to both technical staff and leadership in a clear, evidence-based manner.
